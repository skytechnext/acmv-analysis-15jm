The system is genuinely undersized — this is not a maintenance niggle. The home's own Daikin heat-load study calls for 128.9 kW of cooling, but only 106.3 kW of condenser capacity was installed — and that 106 kW is the nominal rating, which de-rates further because the design chases a very cold 23 °C indoor target. The 1st storey (living/dining/kitchen) and 2nd storey (bedrooms) are the worst hit, with only three-quarters of the cooling they need. On a hot afternoon the compressors run flat-out yet still cannot pull these rooms down to setpoint — exactly the "too hot" symptom the owner reports. Undersized indoor units, an aggressive design setpoint, unshaded full-height glazing, and bedrooms with no dehumidified fresh air all compound it.

All load figures are taken directly from the project's DK-BIM "Heat Load Calculation – Summary Report (Revision 0)"; all equipment figures from the "ACMV As-Build Drawing RevA". Nominal VRV capacities are manufacturer ratings at standard conditions and should be de-rated for the tropical / low-setpoint design point.

Why the design basis matters

  • 23 °C setpoint: ~2 °C colder than the usual 24–25 °C residential design. It both raises the calculated load and lowers the achievable VRV capacity (coils run at a lower evaporating temperature), so the equipment shortfall is effectively worse than the headline 18%.
  • Full-height tinted single glazing (U 5.89, SC 0.70, curtain "None"): the single biggest gain path. Unshaded glass admits both strong solar radiation and conductive heat, and it radiates onto occupants — so rooms can feel hot near the glass even when air temperature is at setpoint.
  • Bedrooms = "No Outdoor Air intake": no dehumidified fresh air is supplied upstairs; toilet exhaust fans (TEF) then depressurise the rooms and pull in warm, humid outdoor air, pushing up humidity — and high humidity reads as "hot".

5Root-Cause Diagnosis: Why It Feels Too Hot

Ranked by impact. The first two are capacity problems (the system can't make enough cold); the rest are why the cold that is made doesn't translate into comfort.

1

Condensers undersized on the 1st & 2nd storeys

Living/dining/kitchen (S2) and bedrooms (S3) have only 75% of required cooling. On hot afternoons the compressors saturate at 100% and room temperature drifts above setpoint. This is the primary driver of the complaint.

2

Indoor fan-coils smaller than room peak loads

Wet Kitchen (49%), Bedroom 3 (56%), Bedroom 2 (60%), WIW (60%). Even with a healthy condenser, an undersized coil + airflow can't move enough heat out of the room — these spaces would lag regardless.

3

Very cold 23 °C design setpoint

Designing (and running) at 23 °C both inflates the load and de-rates VRV capacity. The same equipment that's 18% short at 23 °C would be much closer to adequate at 24–25 °C.

4

Unshaded full-height single-glazed glass (U 5.89, SC 0.70)

The dominant heat-gain path and a radiant-comfort problem: occupants feel heat radiating off the glass even when air temperature is correct. No curtains/blinds were modelled or (apparently) fitted.

5

No dehumidified fresh air to bedrooms + exhaust-fan depressurisation

Bedrooms have "No Outdoor Air intake"; toilet exhaust fans pull in hot humid air. High humidity makes rooms feel hot and sticky even at the temperature setpoint, and loads the coils with latent heat.

6

Tall ceilings (3.5–4.4 m) → stratification

Large volumes with warm air pooling in the occupied zone if supply throw/return is not commissioned for the height. Worsens the "hot" feeling at head height.

Before spending on new equipment — rule out faults first. An undersized system and a faulty system produce the same symptom. A low refrigerant charge / R410A leak, a tripped or non-running compressor, fouled coils/filters, or condensers short-cycling hot air on the roof can each strip 10–30% of capacity. A 1–2 day commissioning audit must come first (see Action Plan).

6Solution Options — Compared

Three packages, from "tune what's there" to "fix it properly". They are cumulative: A should be done first regardless, and is a prerequisite for deciding how far B/C need to go.

Option A — Optimise & Commission

Lowest cost · days
Effort ●○○○○
Comfort gain ●●○○○
  • Full VRV commissioning audit: refrigerant charge / leak test, compressor & fan operation, error logs.
  • Clean coils & filters; set fans to High; verify thermostat sensing & calibration.
  • Check roof condenser layout for hot-air recirculation / short-cycling and clearances.
  • Add solar film + blinds/curtains to full-height glazing.
  • Reset realistic setpoint (24–25 °C); balance airflow.

Recovers "lost" capacity and cuts load — may be enough for the milder rooms, and quantifies the true remaining shortfall.

Option B — Targeted Retrofit

Medium cost · weeks
Effort ●●●○○
Comfort gain ●●●●○
  • Everything in A, plus:
  • Upsize the worst indoor units (Wet Kitchen, Bedrooms 1–3, WIW) to match room peaks.
  • Split the shared Dining+Dry-Kitchen coil into two correctly-sized units.
  • Add Low-E solar film (target SC ≈ 0.30) on all full-height glass — cuts solar gain ~50%.
  • Add ceiling fans / destratification for the tall rooms; supplementary dehumidifiers to bedrooms.

Best value-for-money: directly fixes the starved rooms and shaves load so the existing condensers cope on more days. Won't fully cure a peak-day condenser shortfall.

Option C — Proper Re-Sizing

Highest cost · project

Cost bands are relative orders of magnitude, not quotes — firm pricing needs a site survey and supplier RFQs. Effort/comfort ratings are qualitative engineering judgement.

7Recommended Action Plan

Commissioning & fault audit (Week 1). Before any spend on equipment, have the contractor verify refrigerant charge/leaks, compressor & fan operation, coil/filter condition, thermostat sensing, and roof condenser airflow. Log delivered capacity vs. nameplate per system.
Data-log the worst rooms (Week 1–2). Put temperature/RH loggers in Wet Kitchen, Bedrooms 1–3, Living and Dining over hot afternoons to confirm which rooms actually drift and by how much — this separates true undersizing from faults and right-sizes the fix.
Quick wins (Week 2). Solar film + blinds on full-height glazing, fans to High, setpoint to 24–25 °C, airflow balanced. Re-measure — many rooms may now satisfy.
Targeted retrofit (Option B). Upsize the confirmed-starved indoor units and split the Dining/Dry-Kitchen coil. Add bedroom dehumidification / fresh air.
Decide on condenser re-sizing (Option C). If logged data still shows the 1st/2nd storey condensers saturating on peak days, plan RXQ12→RXQ16 (S2) and RXQ10→RXQ14 (S3), or re-zoning, as a scheduled works package.
Re-issue the heat-load calc at the agreed setpoint. Re-run DK-BIM at 24–25 °C with shading/blinds credited and correct LED lighting watts, to confirm the final equipment selection and avoid over- or under-shooting.
